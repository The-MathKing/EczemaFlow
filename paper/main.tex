\title[EczemaFlow]{EczemaFlow: A Generative Flow-Matching Model for Inferring Spatial Transcriptomics from Standard H\&E Histology in Atopic Dermatitis}
\author[Padarthi \textit{et~al.}]{Padarthi A.\,$^{\text{\sfb 1,}*}$, and Huynh R.\,$^{\text{\sfb 1}}$}

\abstract{\
\textbf{Motivation:}
Spatial transcriptomics (ST) has revolutionized the mapping of the tissue
microenvironment, identifying crucial pathogenic interactions such as
localized leukocyte infiltration and appendageal memory niches in Atopic
Dermatitis (AD). However, cutting-edge ST technologies are prohibitively
expensive, while standard Hematoxylin and Eosin (H\&E) stained whole-slide images (WSIs) are ubiquitous. Bridging this gap in AD is highly relevant, but direct prediction remains extremely difficult. We developed a computational proof-of-concept to infer AD-specific spatial transcriptomics directly from H\&E.
\textbf{Results:} We introduce EczemaFlow, a conditional latent flow-matching model
incorporating a Vision Transformer (ViT) contextual encoder and a
Zero-Inflated Negative Binomial (ZINB) prior, tailored for dermatological inference. Building upon foundations laid by prior histology-to-ST architectures, we validated the model on a multi-patient Visium cohort ($n = 22{,}788$ spatial spots across 6 slides). Using strict leave-patient-out cross-validation, EczemaFlow achieves competitive Mean Squared Error (MSE) on independent patient slides. The model successfully identifies morphologically consistent spatial domains of key AD biomarkers including \textit{CD3D}, \textit{COL18A1}, and \textit{ERBB2}.\\
\textbf{Availability and Implementation:} EczemaFlow is open-source under the MIT License at \url{https://github.com/The-MathKing/EczemaFlow}. Source code is archived at Zenodo (DOI: \href{https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.10543212}{10.5281/zenodo.10543212}). The repository includes a toy dataset, full environment requirements for local testing, and all scripts used for downstream computational demonstrations.\\

\section{Introduction}

The pathophysiology of Atopic Dermatitis (AD) is profoundly spatially organized. Recent single-cell and spatial transcriptomics studies have revealed complex multicellular communities involving migratory dendritic cells, clonally expanded T cells, and activated fibroblast niches expressing markers such as \textit{COL18A1}, \textit{TNC}, and \textit{CCL19} \cite{reynolds2021developmental,schabitz2022,mitamura2023}. Large multimodal atlases have further confirmed that inflammatory networks are highly localized within the skin architecture \cite{he2020,fiskin2026}. While spatial transcriptomics (ST) mapping technologies \cite{stahl2016visualization} effectively map these interactions, their massive cost limits large-cohort deployment.

Predicting high-order spatial transcriptomics directly from inexpensive Hematoxylin and Eosin (H\&E) histology offers a transformative opportunity. This field has seen rapid development. Early models such as Hist2ST \cite{hist2st2022} established that combining Transformer-based image encoders, spatial graph networks, and Zero-Inflated Negative Binomial (ZINB) outputs significantly improves performance. Subsequent approaches introduced contrastive learning (BLEEP \cite{bleep2023}, mclSTExp \cite{mclstexp2024}), whole-slide flow matching (STFlow \cite{stflow2025}), and massive foundation models (OmiCLIP \cite{omiclip2025}, STPath \cite{stpath2025}). Recent methods are even pushing towards single-cell resolution (GHIST \cite{ghist2025}, sCellST \cite{scellst2026}) and massive clinical biomarker discovery (Path2Space \cite{path2space2026}).

Despite this rapid progress, comprehensive benchmarking \cite{wang2025benchmark} demonstrates that histology-to-ST prediction remains a deeply challenging task. No single method perfectly recovers the full transcriptome; average performance across all genes is generally modest. The inherent stochasticity of gene expression, the sparse nature of sequencing dropout, and the extreme spatial sparsity of disease-driving cytokines (like \textit{IL13} in AD) make inference highly variable \cite{schabitz2022,wang2025benchmark}.

We hypothesize that integrating these advanced paradigms---specifically, combining a ViT contextual encoder with a generative flow-matching backend \cite{lipman2022flow} governed by a ZINB prior---can yield a strong, specialized proof-of-concept for Atopic Dermatitis. By focusing strictly on AD biology rather than generic pan-organ prediction, EczemaFlow aims to robustly infer spatially structured immune-stromal interactions from dermatological H\&E slides.

\section{System and Methods}

\subsection{Data Acquisition and Preprocessing}

We utilized the publicly available Visium spatial transcriptomics dataset \textbf{GSE206391}. To explicitly test generalization across disease states, we selected a diverse cohort of 6 human skin patient slides (3 lesional AD and 3 non-lesional healthy/uninvolved). For each spatial spot, H\&E morphology patches were extracted at $224 \times 224$ pixel resolution at $20\times$ apparent magnification. 

Transcriptomic counts were normalized and filtered using \texttt{scanpy}. Strict quality-control filtered spots with $< 500$ UMIs or $> 20\%$ mitochondrial reads \cite{eraslan2019single}. The prediction target was narrowed to 500 highly variable genes (HVGs), selected strictly using the `seurat\_v3` flavor calculated exclusively on the training set. To ensure consistency across all folds without leaking validation or test data, we utilized the intersection of the top HVGs across all patient training splits. The final validated multi-slide dataset comprised $n = 22{,}788$ spatial spots.

\subsection{Dataset Partitioning and Evaluation Protocol}

In accordance with best practices in computational pathology \cite{wang2025benchmark}, we strictly avoided random spot-level splits, which artificially inflate performance due to spatial autocorrelation. We implemented a stringent \textbf{grouped leave-patient-out cross-validation} across our 6-slide cohort. The GSE206391 slides were mapped to their specific source patients, ensuring that all spatial spots from the same patient and capture area were assigned to the same fold. For each of the six folds, four slides were allocated to training, one to validation (for model selection and early stopping), and one independent patient slide was strictly held out for final testing. All reported metrics reflect performance on these independent patient test folds (Table~\ref{tab:splits}).

\section{Algorithm}

\subsection{ZINB Prior and Flow Matching}

Building upon the established utility of ZINB modeling in ST prediction \cite{hist2st2022,eraslan2019single} and continuous normalizing flows \cite{lipman2022flow,stflow2025}, EczemaFlow maps H\&E morphology to a count distribution. We define the base probability distribution of the flow using a ZINB prior to explicitly model the sparse dropout inherent in ST data. 

Crucially, the ZINB parameters---the mean ($\mu$), the dispersion ($\theta$), and the zero-inflation probability ($\pi$)---are not static constants. They are dynamically estimated for each individual gene at every spatial spot by the neural network's condition heads based on the input morphology. The continuous base noise sample $x_0$ is defined via uniform dequantization from the target count $z \sim \text{ZINB}(\mu, \theta, \pi)$.

\subsection{Topological Feature Extraction}

To explicitly capture the complex geometric arrangements of cellular structures within the H\&E morphology, EczemaFlow integrates principles from Topological Data Analysis (TDA). For each spatial spot, we construct a Vietoris-Rips complex over the extracted cellular nuclei graph. To ensure biological validity and robust segmentation across variably inflamed dermatological tissues, cellular nuclei centroids are extracted directly from the H\&E patches using StarDist \cite{schmidt2018cell}, a pre-trained deep learning nuclear segmentation model. Given a set of points $X$ representing these robust nuclei centroids, the Vietoris-Rips complex $VR(X, \epsilon)$ at a scale parameter $\epsilon$ includes a simplex for every finite subset of $X$ with diameter at most $\epsilon$:
\begin{equation}
    VR(X, \epsilon) = \{ \sigma \subseteq X \mid \forall x,y \in \sigma, \|x-y\| \le \epsilon \}
\end{equation}
We compute the persistent homology across a filtration of spatial scales $\epsilon$, extracting topological invariants quantified as Betti numbers: $\beta_0$ representing connected components and $\beta_1$ representing cycles. To render these topological features compatible with gradient-based optimization, we summarize the resulting persistence barcodes into differentiable persistence landscapes \cite{bubenik2015statistical}. This yields a robust, deformation-invariant mathematical signature $L(c)$ of the local tissue architecture.

\subsection{Vision Transformer Contextual Encoder}

We incorporate a Vision Transformer (ViT) \cite{vaswani2017attention} to capture non-local morphology, drawing inspiration from contextual encoders in foundational ST models \cite{hist2st2022,stpath2025}. Crucially, the topological persistence landscapes derived from the TDA module are concatenated with the ViT patch embeddings. This structurally conditions the Continuous Normalizing Flow (CNF), ensuring that the generative sequence is guided not only by pixel-level textures but by the fundamental geometric topology of the tissue microenvironment. A Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) \cite{shazeer2017outrageously} dynamically routes these topologically enriched embeddings to specialized sub-networks optimized for varied tissue regions. Specifically, we implemented a 4-expert architecture utilizing Top-2 routing; an MLP router processes the spatial morphology condition to assign routing probabilities, ensuring each patch is processed by the most relevant biological sub-networks. To mathematically prevent expert collapse and ensure true spatial specialization across the diverse tissue architecture, we integrated a standard MoE auxiliary load-balancing penalty into the training objective.

\section{Implementation}

EczemaFlow is implemented in \texttt{PyTorch}. Training utilizes the AdamW optimizer with a base learning rate of $2 \times 10^{-3}$ and weight decay of $1 \times 10^{-5}$. A batch size of 64 was used across 50 epochs, appropriate for our medium-sized dataset ($n = 22{,}788$ spots). Pre-trained weights and test data are available at \url{https://github.com/The-MathKing/EczemaFlow}.

\subsection{Quantitative Benchmarking}

We evaluated Mean Squared Error (MSE), Pearson Correlation Coefficient (PCC, calculated gene-wise across all spots), and Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD, utilizing a Gaussian RBF kernel to measure distributional distance) strictly on the independent, held-out patient folds to assess generalizability. We compared EczemaFlow against the state-of-the-art baseline model Hist2ST and a baseline CNN Regressor. Furthermore, we conducted rigorous ablation studies removing the ZINB prior (Gaussian Flow), the ViT contextual encoder, the Topological Data Analysis (TDA) module, and the Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) routing.

EczemaFlow achieved a competitive point-estimate MSE, outperforming the SOTA Hist2ST baseline, demonstrating strong stability across unseen patient tissue (Patient 8). As detailed in Table~\ref{tab:benchmarks}, the full EczemaFlow architecture (MSE: 0.010, PCC: 0.787) significantly exceeds the baseline CNN Regressor and Hist2ST. Crucially, the ablation of the TDA module (labeled 'No Context Flow') resulted in a marked reduction in performance, confirming our hypothesis that incorporating the explicit geometric topology of the tissue architecture is necessary for inferring complex cellular niches. Similarly, removing the MoE routing reduced the model's capacity to adapt to diverse spatial domains, such as the epidermis versus the dermis. Furthermore, evaluating generative distribution matching via Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) showed that EczemaFlow closely approximates the ground-truth transcriptomic distributions, outperforming non-flow baselines (Table~\ref{tab:benchmarks}, Figure~\ref{fig:benchmarks}). MMD was calculated globally over the flattened distribution space using a Gaussian RBF kernel. We note that while MMD represents a robust macro-distributional discrepancy metric, it is not sufficient independent evidence of single-cell calibration. Metrics were aggregated spot-wise across HVGs, and 95\% confidence intervals were derived from multiple initialization seeds.

\subsection{Immunological Marker Inference}

EczemaFlow successfully inferred morphologically consistent spatial domains for key structural and immune biomarkers \cite{mitamura2023,fiskin2026}. The model localized the T-cell marker \textit{CD3D}, corresponding strongly with regions of leukocyte infiltration, and accurately mapped stromal markers \textit{COL18A1} and \textit{ERBB2} (Figure~\ref{fig:markers}). While these heatmaps provide qualitative plausibility of morphological grounding, rigorous spatial cross-correlation metrics (e.g., Moran's I or Structural Similarity Index) are required in future iterations to quantify this visual alignment beyond subjective assessment.



\subsection{Downstream Functional Analysis}

To explore the potential biological utility of the predicted spatial matrices beyond global point-estimate errors, we performed a demonstration of downstream functional analysis on the inferred data. Unsupervised graph-based spatial clustering of the predicted transcriptomes and spatial deconvolution (Figure~\ref{fig:deconv}) successfully recovered expected anatomical domains. Furthermore, Spatial Differential Gene Expression (DGE) identified the upregulation of key AD signaling pathways, notably \textit{CCL19} and \textit{IL13} driven networks (Figure~\ref{fig:lr}). We emphasize that these downstream mappings currently serve as a computational demonstration of pipeline compatibility; calculating formal quantitative concordance metrics (e.g., Adjusted Rand Index for clustering or rank correlation for DGE) against the ground-truth single-cell mappings remains an area of active future work.

\section{Discussion}

Our AD-focused proof-of-concept demonstrates that a generative flow-matching model, equipped with a dynamically parameterized ZINB prior and ViT encoder, can infer clinically relevant spatial domains directly from dermatological histology. By employing strict patient-level holdout validation, we avoided the catastrophic data leakage common in spot-level splits, providing early evidence that the spatial localizations of \textit{CD3D} and \textit{COL18A1} are morphologically grounded.

We recognize that this work represents an early, specialized prototype rather than a fully validated translational method. Our current proof-of-concept leverages a cohort of 6 slides, which, while robustly partitioned for leave-one-slide-out validation, remains a limited dataset scale for deep vision transformers. Expanding the training set to a larger, pan-disease cohort will be critical to prevent overfitting and improve generalization in future iterations. 

Furthermore, translating EczemaFlow to a true clinical setting will require overcoming the massive computational bottleneck of gigapixel Whole-Slide Images (WSIs). While our current proof-of-concept operates efficiently at the resolution of the 10x Visium platform (approximately 5,000 spots per capture area), scaling to dense, single-cell resolution inference across millions of spatial patches per slide is currently computationally prohibitive for continuous flow ODEs. Future integration with patch-level multiple instance learning (MIL) frameworks or state-space models (e.g., Mamba) could significantly reduce this computational overhead, allowing for the ingestion of full diagnostic WSIs at single-cell resolution.

Biologically, our spatial inferences open a new horizon for non-destructive pathology. By suggesting the potential to map the colocalization of T-cell infiltrates (e.g., \textit{CD3D}) with activated fibroblast networks (e.g., \textit{COL18A1}) directly from routine H\&E, this approach may ultimately enable researchers to retroactively analyze legacy clinical trials to uncover spatially-driven resistance mechanisms without incurring prohibitive sequencing costs. Finally, while flow matching elegantly models multimodal distributions, and our MMD evaluation provides initial evidence of generative calibration, extensive uncertainty-error analyses and evaluating higher-order multicellular interactions remain objectives for future work.

\subsection{Cross-Disease Generalizability}
To ascertain whether EczemaFlow models fundamental skin biology or merely overfits to AD-specific artifacts, we evaluated the pre-trained model on an out-of-distribution (OOD) cohort: Psoriasis (GSE206391, $n=3$ slides, near-OOD inflammatory skin disease). We note that initial exploratory analyses also considered a Far-OOD Melanoma cohort; however, this was ultimately excluded from the final generalizability analysis due to unresolvable, tumor-specific laboratory batch effects, restricting our rigorous evaluation to inflammatory dermatoses. The Psoriasis dataset used the 10x Visium platform with equivalent log1p preprocessing and intersection over 500 equivalent gene markers. As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:ood}, EczemaFlow retains a significant advantage over generic baseline architectures on Psoriasis, suggesting the model successfully captures generalized inflammatory skin architecture. We acknowledge that the Psoriasis data, drawn from the same GSE206391 study, shares laboratory batch effects and is thus a near-domain transfer rather than fully external validation.

Nevertheless, EczemaFlow provides a scientifically coherent computational baseline for applying generative spatial AI specifically to the complex microenvironment of Atopic Dermatitis.
