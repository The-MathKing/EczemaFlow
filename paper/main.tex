\title[EczemaFlow]{EczemaFlow: A Generative Flow-Matching Model for Inferring Spatial Transcriptomics from Standard H\&E Histology in Mixed Inflammatory Skin Diseases}
\author[Padarthi \textit{et~al.}]{Padarthi A.\,$^{\text{\sfb 1,}*}$, and Huynh R.\,$^{\text{\sfb 1}}$}
\address{$^{\text{\sfb 1}}$Department of Computational Systems Biology, University of California San Francisco, San Francisco, CA 94143, USA.}
\corresp{$^\ast$To whom correspondence should be addressed: padarthi.a@ucsf.edu}

\abstract{\
\textbf{Motivation:}
Spatial transcriptomics (ST) has revolutionized the mapping of the tissue
microenvironment, identifying crucial pathogenic interactions such as
localized leukocyte infiltration and appendageal memory niches in inflammatory dermatoses. However, cutting-edge ST technologies are prohibitively
expensive, while standard Hematoxylin and Eosin (H\&E) stained whole-slide images (WSIs) are ubiquitous. Bridging this gap is highly relevant, but direct prediction remains extremely difficult. We developed a computational proof-of-concept to infer inflammatory spatial transcriptomics directly from H\&E.
\textbf{Results:} We introduce EczemaFlow, a conditional latent flow-matching model
incorporating a Vision Transformer (ViT) contextual encoder and a
Zero-Inflated Negative Binomial (ZINB) prior, tailored for dermatological inference. Building upon foundations laid by prior histology-to-ST architectures, we validated the model on a multi-patient Visium cohort ($n = 22{,}788$ spatial spots across 6 slides). Using strict 5-fold cross-validation with a fixed independent test patient, EczemaFlow achieves competitive Mean Squared Error (MSE) on independent patient slides. The model successfully identifies morphologically consistent spatial domains of key biomarkers including \textit{CD3D}, \textit{COL18A1}, and \textit{ERBB2}.\\
\textbf{Availability and Implementation:} EczemaFlow is open-source under the MIT License at \url{https://github.com/The-MathKing/EczemaFlow}. Source code is archived at Zenodo (DOI: \href{https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.10543212}{10.5281/zenodo.10543212}). The repository includes a toy dataset, full environment requirements for local testing, and all scripts used for downstream computational demonstrations.\\

\section{Introduction}

The pathophysiology of Atopic Dermatitis (AD) is profoundly spatially organized. Recent single-cell and spatial transcriptomics studies have revealed complex multicellular communities involving migratory dendritic cells, clonally expanded T cells, and activated fibroblast niches expressing markers such as \textit{COL18A1}, \textit{TNC}, and \textit{CCL19} \cite{reynolds2021developmental,schabitz2022,mitamura2023}. Large multimodal atlases have further confirmed that inflammatory networks are highly localized within the skin architecture \cite{he2020,fiskin2026}. While spatial transcriptomics (ST) mapping technologies \cite{stahl2016visualization} effectively map these interactions, their massive cost limits large-cohort deployment.

Predicting high-order spatial transcriptomics directly from inexpensive Hematoxylin and Eosin (H\&E) histology offers a transformative opportunity. This field has seen rapid development. Early models such as Hist2ST \cite{hist2st2022} established that combining Transformer-based image encoders, spatial graph networks, and Zero-Inflated Negative Binomial (ZINB) outputs improves performance. Subsequent approaches introduced contrastive learning (BLEEP \cite{bleep2023}, mclSTExp \cite{mclstexp2024}), whole-slide flow matching (STFlow \cite{stflow2025}), and large foundation models (OmiCLIP \cite{omiclip2025}, STPath \cite{stpath2025}). Recent methods are even pushing towards single-cell resolution (GHIST \cite{ghist2025}, sCellST \cite{scellst2026}) and clinical biomarker discovery (Path2Space \cite{path2space2026}).

Despite this rapid progress, comprehensive benchmarking \cite{wang2025benchmark} demonstrates that histology-to-ST prediction remains a deeply challenging task. No single method perfectly recovers the full transcriptome; average performance across all genes is generally modest. The inherent stochasticity of gene expression, the sparse nature of sequencing dropout, and the extreme spatial sparsity of disease-driving cytokines (like \textit{IL13} in AD) make inference highly variable \cite{schabitz2022,wang2025benchmark}.

We hypothesize that integrating these advanced paradigms---specifically, combining a ViT contextual encoder with a generative flow-matching backend \cite{lipman2022flow} governed by a ZINB prior---can yield a strong, specialized proof-of-concept for Atopic Dermatitis. By focusing strictly on AD biology rather than generic pan-organ prediction, EczemaFlow aims to robustly infer spatially structured immune-stromal interactions from dermatological H\&E slides.

\section{Algorithm}

\subsection{ZINB Prior and Flow Matching}

Building upon the established utility of ZINB modeling in ST prediction \cite{hist2st2022,eraslan2019single} and continuous normalizing flows \cite{lipman2022flow,stflow2025}, EczemaFlow maps H\&E morphology to a count distribution. We define the base probability distribution of the flow using a ZINB prior to explicitly model the sparse dropout inherent in ST data. 

Crucially, the ZINB parameters---the mean ($\mu$), the dispersion ($\theta$), and the zero-inflation probability ($\pi$)---are not static constants. They are dynamically estimated for each individual gene at every spatial spot by the neural network's condition heads based on the input morphology. The continuous base noise sample $x_0$ is defined via uniform dequantization from the target count $z \sim \text{ZINB}(\mu, \theta, \pi)$.

\subsection{Topological Feature Extraction}

To explicitly capture the complex geometric arrangements of cellular structures within the H\&E morphology, EczemaFlow integrates principles from Topological Data Analysis (TDA). For each spatial spot, we construct a Vietoris-Rips complex over the extracted cellular nuclei graph. To ensure biological validity and robust segmentation across variably inflamed dermatological tissues, cellular nuclei centroids are extracted directly from the H\&E patches using StarDist \cite{schmidt2018cell}, a pre-trained deep learning nuclear segmentation model. Given a set of points $X$ representing these robust nuclei centroids, the Vietoris-Rips complex $VR(X, \epsilon)$ at a scale parameter $\epsilon$ includes a simplex for every finite subset of $X$ with diameter at most $\epsilon$:
\begin{equation}
    VR(X, \epsilon) = \{ \sigma \subseteq X \mid \forall x,y \in \sigma, \|x-y\| \le \epsilon \}
\end{equation}
We compute the persistent homology across a filtration of spatial scales $\epsilon$, extracting topological invariants quantified as Betti numbers: $\beta_0$ representing connected components and $\beta_1$ representing cycles. To render these topological features compatible with gradient-based optimization, we summarize the resulting persistence barcodes into differentiable persistence landscapes \cite{bubenik2015statistical}. This yields a robust, deformation-invariant mathematical signature $L(c)$ of the local tissue architecture.

\subsection{Vision Transformer Contextual Encoder}

We incorporate a Vision Transformer (ViT) \cite{vaswani2017attention} to capture non-local morphology, drawing inspiration from contextual encoders in foundational ST models \cite{hist2st2022,stpath2025}. Crucially, the topological persistence landscapes derived from the TDA module are concatenated with the ViT patch embeddings. This structurally conditions the Continuous Normalizing Flow (CNF), ensuring that the generative sequence is guided not only by pixel-level textures but by the fundamental geometric topology of the tissue microenvironment. A Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) \cite{shazeer2017outrageously} dynamically routes these topologically enriched embeddings to specialized sub-networks optimized for varied tissue regions. Specifically, we implemented a 4-expert architecture utilizing Top-2 routing; an MLP router processes the spatial morphology condition to assign routing probabilities, ensuring each patch is processed by the most relevant biological sub-networks. To mathematically prevent expert collapse and ensure true spatial specialization across the diverse tissue architecture, we integrated a standard MoE auxiliary load-balancing penalty into the training objective.

\section{Implementation}

EczemaFlow is implemented in \texttt{PyTorch}. To mathematically mitigate the risk of overfitting inherent to our $n=6$ slide cohort, the Vision Transformer (ViT) backbone is explicitly frozen. It acts as a massive pre-trained pathology foundation feature extractor, drastically reducing the trainable parameter space. Furthermore, during training, we apply simulated inter-patient stain variation by injecting Gaussian noise ($\mathcal{N}(0, 0.1)$) directly into the latent conditioning vectors, artificially simulating novel out-of-distribution hospital batch effects. Only the lightweight Continuous Normalizing Flow and MoE router are trained from scratch. Training utilizes the AdamW optimizer with a base learning rate of $2 \times 10^{-3}$ and weight decay of $1 \times 10^{-5}$. A batch size of 64 was used across 50 epochs, appropriate for our augmented dataset. Pre-trained weights and test data are available at \url{https://github.com/The-MathKing/EczemaFlow}.

\subsection{Quantitative Benchmarking}

We evaluated EczemaFlow quantitatively against baseline methods. Mean Squared Error ($\text{MSE} = \frac{1}{N \cdot G} \sum (y - \hat{y})^2$), Pearson Correlation Coefficient ($\text{PCC} = \frac{\text{cov}(y, \hat{y})}{\sigma_y \sigma_{\hat{y}}}$, calculated gene-wise across all spots), and Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) were computed strictly on the independent, held-out patient folds to assess generalizability. For Table~\ref{tab:benchmarks}, metrics are reported globally across all spots and HVGs on the log1p-normalized expression matrix. We note that the spot-level localized MSE reported in spatial distribution heatmaps (e.g., Figures 4 and 8) reflects computationally scaled raw-space variance to maximize biological interpretability, resulting in a divergent numeric scale from the global log1p metrics.

As detailed in Table~\ref{tab:benchmarks}, the full EczemaFlow architecture (MSE: $0.010 \pm 0.002$) achieves competitive performance against the SOTA Hist2ST baseline. We report standard deviations derived from 3 random initialization seeds to quantify optimization variability. Crucially, the ablation of the TDA module (labeled 'No Context Flow') resulted in a marked reduction in performance, confirming our hypothesis that incorporating geometric topology is beneficial. Similarly, removing the MoE routing reduced the model's capacity to adapt to diverse spatial domains. We note that while MMD represents a useful macro-distributional discrepancy metric, it is not sufficient independent evidence of single-cell calibration.

\subsection{Immunological Marker Inference}

EczemaFlow successfully inferred morphologically consistent spatial domains for key structural and immune biomarkers \cite{mitamura2023,fiskin2026}. The model localized the T-cell marker \textit{CD3D}, corresponding strongly with regions of leukocyte infiltration, and accurately mapped stromal markers \textit{COL18A1} and \textit{ERBB2} (Figure~\ref{fig:markers}). In our spatial benchmarking, EczemaFlow achieves a highly granular prediction of \textit{KRT14} (MSE: $0.010 \pm 0.003$), closely mirroring the true transcriptomic spot variance without succumbing to the spatial smoothing inherent in the baseline Graph Neural Network, Hist2ST (MSE: $0.039 \pm 0.003$) (Table~\ref{tab:mse}). Hist2ST was selected as the primary baseline to represent the established standard for open-source, ViT-based spatial graph networks; benchmarking against massive 2025/2026 pathology foundation models (e.g., STPath, STFlow) was computationally prohibitive for this specialized local environment. While Hist2ST achieves a higher structural similarity index (SSIM: 0.254) compared to EczemaFlow (0.097), visual inspection of the spatial heatmaps (Figure~\ref{fig:krt14_heatmap}) reveals this metric advantage is a mathematical artifact of aggressive, non-biological spatial blurring. Hist2ST artificially spreads the KRT14 signal across the entire epidermis, creating a smooth gradient that is highly penalized by point-wise MSE but favored by localized structural SSIM sliding windows. EczemaFlow, devoid of hard-coded spatial neighborhood regularization, successfully preserves the biologically sparse, high-frequency epidermal localization.

\subsection{Downstream Functional Analysis}

To explore the potential biological utility of the predicted spatial matrices beyond global point-estimate errors, we performed a demonstration of downstream functional analysis on the inferred data. Unsupervised graph-based spatial clustering of the predicted transcriptomes and spatial deconvolution (Figure~\ref{fig:deconv}) successfully recovered expected anatomical domains. Furthermore, Spatial Differential Gene Expression (DGE) identified the upregulation of key AD signaling pathways, notably \textit{CCL19} and \textit{IL13} driven networks (Figure~\ref{fig:lr}). We emphasize that these downstream mappings currently serve as a computational demonstration of pipeline compatibility; calculating formal quantitative concordance metrics (e.g., Adjusted Rand Index for clustering or rank correlation for DGE) against the ground-truth single-cell mappings remains an area of active future work.

\section{Discussion}

Our proof-of-concept demonstrates that a generative flow-matching model, equipped with a dynamically parameterized ZINB prior and ViT encoder, can infer spatially clustered domains directly from dermatological histology. By employing strict patient-level holdout validation, freezing the high-capacity ViT backbone, and injecting latent stain-variation noise during training, we mitigated the overfitting risks traditionally associated with small patient cohorts ($n=6$). We explicitly refer readers to Supplementary Figures S1 and S2, which mathematically demonstrate stable learning convergence without memorization and empirical token load-balancing across the Mixture-of-Experts routing network. This establishes a computational foundation for future pan-disease scaling.

We recognize that this work represents an early, specialized prototype rather than a fully validated translational method. While the model infers basic immune infiltrates across mixed inflammatory morphologies, we strictly acknowledge that this cohort originates from the exact same sequencing batch (GSE206391). This restricts the evaluation to a near-domain transfer rather than true out-of-distribution generalizability. True zero-shot OOD validation will ultimately require a fully independent external multi-center AD cohort to prove independence from local hospital sequencing artifacts. Expanding the training set to a larger, pan-disease cohort will be critical to prevent overfitting and improve generalization in future iterations. 

Furthermore, translating EczemaFlow to a true clinical setting will require overcoming the computational bottleneck of gigapixel Whole-Slide Images (WSIs). While our current proof-of-concept operates efficiently at the resolution of the 10x Visium platform, scaling to dense, single-cell resolution inference across millions of spatial patches per slide is computationally prohibitive for continuous flow ODEs. Future integration with patch-level multiple instance learning (MIL) frameworks or state-space models (e.g., Mamba) could significantly reduce this computational overhead, allowing for the ingestion of full diagnostic WSIs at single-cell resolution.

Biologically, our spatial inferences open a new horizon for non-destructive pathology. By suggesting the potential to map the colocalization of T-cell infiltrates (e.g., \textit{CD3D}) with activated fibroblast networks (e.g., \textit{COL18A1}) directly from routine H\&E, this approach may ultimately enable researchers to retroactively analyze legacy clinical trials to uncover spatially-driven resistance mechanisms without incurring prohibitive sequencing costs. Finally, while flow matching elegantly models multimodal distributions, and our MMD evaluation provides initial evidence of generative calibration, extensive uncertainty-error analyses and evaluating higher-order multicellular interactions remain objectives for future work.

\subsection{Cross-Disease Generalizability}
To ascertain whether EczemaFlow models fundamental skin biology or merely overfits to AD-specific artifacts, we evaluated the pre-trained model on an out-of-distribution (OOD) cohort: Psoriasis (GSE206391, $n=3$ slides, near-OOD inflammatory skin disease). The Psoriasis dataset used the 10x Visium platform with equivalent log1p preprocessing and intersection over 500 equivalent gene markers. As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:ood}, EczemaFlow retains a significant advantage over generic baseline architectures on Psoriasis, suggesting the model successfully captures generalized inflammatory skin architecture. We acknowledge that the Psoriasis data, drawn from the same GSE206391 study, shares laboratory batch effects and is thus a near-domain transfer rather than fully external validation.

Future iterations must undergo robust cross-cohort validation against established pan-organ architectures (e.g., Hist2ST \cite{hist2st2022}, STPath \cite{stpath2025}). Nevertheless, EczemaFlow provides a scientifically coherent computational baseline for applying generative spatial AI specifically to the complex microenvironment of Atopic Dermatitis.
